Tuning Lab

Pick a target tuning — the needle guides every string toward it.

New strings drift — stretch them gently and retune a few times. Temperature changes and capos knock tuning out too; a 30-second check before practice is normal.

Capo Check

Clamp the capo just behind the fret, square across the neck. Then pick each string once, thickest first.

Capo Compass

A capo is a transposition machine. Campfire names a key and a shape family — you work out which fret gets you there, clamp it on, and pick each string to prove it.

Fret-12 Intonation Test

The 12th fret should sound exactly one octave above the open string. Pick a string, then follow the two steps. Fret lightly, fingertip just behind the fret.

A string that reads sharp at fret 12 no matter what is an instrument setup issue (saddle position) — a guitar tech's five-minute fix, not your fingers' fault.

Note Hunt

Drone Room

Same seven notes, different home. Campfire holds a root; you improvise over it. Every note you play is named live — chord tone, in-mode, the characteristic tone, or outside.

Earbuds strongly recommended — otherwise the mic hears the drone. Campfire only counts notes that arrive with a fresh pluck, so it ignores its own sustained tone.

Mode Detective

Campfire plays a phrase over a drone. You answer the only way that counts — by playing the one note that gives the mode away. Eight rounds.

The characteristic tone is the single note that separates a mode from plain major or minor. Find it and you've named the mode.

Interval Echo

Campfire plays two notes. You play the second one back. Ten rounds — the drill that turns theory into an ear.

Earbuds help — otherwise the mic hears Campfire's own notes. It waits for silence before listening either way.

—
Melodic Echo

A short phrase, played back by ear. Campfire plays three to five notes from one scale shape; you find them on the neck.

Quality Call

Campfire plays a chord; you answer with the single note that names it — the 3rd for major, the ♭3 for minor, the ♭5 for diminished. The third is where a chord's mood lives.

—
Bass Detective

Campfire plays a four-chord loop. You play back its root movement — just the bass line. Land it and the progression names itself.

Sing Back

Campfire plays a note; you sing it. The same detector reads a voice as easily as a string — and you can't sing a pitch you can't truly hear, which is why this builds an ear faster than anything else here.

Octaves don't matter — sing it wherever it sits comfortably. Tolerance is a generous ±30 cents, nothing here gates your progress, and you can switch it off again in About.

Sixty seconds, two chords, back and forth. Each verified change counts. This is the classic one-minute-changes drill — the fastest way to make chord shapes automatic.

Campfire loops a progression and plays each chord as it arrives. Your job: land a chord tone in every bar. Hitting the 3rd or 7th — the notes that actually spell the harmony — scores double.

About Campfire

A guitar practice app that listens — built from classical signal processing, not AI. Everything runs on this phone: no accounts, no servers, no tracking, no network requests at all. Your audio is analysed live and never recorded or sent anywhere.
